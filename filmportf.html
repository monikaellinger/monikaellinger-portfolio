SHE IS · BOTH SIDES OF THE CAMERA

I'm Monika — a 23-year-old computer science student from the Czech Republic who makes interfaces during the day and storyboards at night. I live where film and product design meet, and I'm convinced that's a more useful place to stand than either one alone.

On the technical side, I design and build: product-grade UX/UI, web and mobile apps, and AI work with CNNs and RNNs. I like tight type, generous whitespace, a clear hierarchy, and the kind of animation that earns its keep.

On the film side, I direct, produce, write, and storyboard. I'm a devout cinephile — Baz Luhrmann, Sofia Coppola, Wong Kar-wai, Greta Gerwig, the A24 house style, anything Roger Deakins touches. I read Sarah J. Maas in Czech, paint landscapes, play golf badly and ski well, and I am, from time to time, the girl on the cliff getting her hair blown sideways by a storm.

I'm open to remote work anywhere, but I'd especially love to get close to the film / TV industry — Netflix, Warner Bros., A24 — on any team where taste and technology have to talk to each other.

The Great
Gatsby, Loud.

On Baz Luhrmann's 2013 rendering of Fitzgerald — why the maximalism is the argument, not the distraction, and why it may be the most correct Gatsby we are going to get.

The complaint most people have about Baz Luhrmann's The Great Gatsby is essentially a complaint about volume: too much music, too much confetti, too many sequins, too much camera. Fitzgerald's prose is restrained, the argument goes, and Luhrmann is not, therefore the film must be wrong. I've always found this argument to be, with respect, a little too quiet to be correct.

Gatsby is not a man. He is a production designer's brief, and Luhrmann is the only director who ever read it that way.

The whole novel is about a man who has invented himself loudly to catch the attention of a woman who prefers soft lighting. Jay Gatsby spends five years building a party so loud that the ambient noise of New York stops registering — and when Daisy finally walks through it, every bit of that volume is for her. Luhrmann understands this. The confetti is not distraction, it's thesis.

Leonardo DiCaprio's performance is the other thing people miss when they complain about the spectacle. He plays Gatsby as someone who is constantly listening for Daisy to arrive from another room. The smile is a door that opens. The eyes flick. He is attentive in the specific way that self-invented people are attentive — because self-invention is a full-time job and you cannot afford to miss a cue.

And then there is the color. Green, of course — the dock light, the envy, the money — but also the champagne golds, the champagne pinks, the champagne everything. Catherine Martin's production design does what Fitzgerald's prose does: it treats wealth as a moral climate, not a decoration.

You do not get to the green light without the confetti. The confetti is the green light.

Is the film perfect? No. The framing device with Nick in a sanatorium is clumsy, and the Jay-Z needle drops have aged into something like affectionate embarrassment. But when Gatsby turns to Nick in the Plaza and says, with his whole chest, that of course you can repeat the past — the film catches, for one second, the thing the novel is actually about: the American habit of believing that enough noise, directed in the right direction, will be heard by the one person you want to hear it. It is not a quiet movie. It was never supposed to be.

★ ★ ★ ★ ½ / 5 4K · DIR. B. LUHRMANN · 2013
